El acento español

Five rules on using the Spanish acento

All sorts of Spanish learners (at all levels) often confess to not being sure about the official rules on when to use and when not to use the Spanish acento («´») in writing. Many will say that the issue doesn't really affect them as they have developed a good instinctive feel for identifying where to put it and where not to put it down on the page. And of course, if that's the case, then there's no problem! But some learners will admit to being a little unsure.

If you're one such Spanish learner, this site is for you! We aim to help remove any uncertainty in your mind by explaining the rules simply and clearly and then giving you the opportunity to practice what you've learned.

Of course, to apply these rules properly, you'll first need to know where to put the stress when saying a word aloud, but that's another part of the language learning task. We leave that up to you, though we recommend that whatever method you choose to learn your Spanish vocabulary you should repeatedly say new words out loud until you're comfortable you're pronouncing them correctly.

There are three purposes to the written acento.

  • It's first and main purpose is to indicate to a reader what syllable to put the stress on a word when speaking it. The general rules of pronunciation of Spanish will usually tell you without the need for an acento, but sometimes it won't; and in such cases you'll need to write your acento on the page (Lessons 1 to 3).
  • Another purpose is to tell certain words that are pronounced the same (and are otherwise spelt the same) apart from one another. (Lesson 4)
  • Finally, in cases where a word contains two or three vowels in succession, a written acento may be needed to tell whether the vowels should be pronounced as a single syllable (i.e. as a dipthong or a tripthong) or broken up into several syllables. (Lesson 5)

Many Spanish materials speak of the acento as a "tilde" (one of the two forms of tilde used in the Spanish language, the other one being the '~' found on top of the 'n' shape to form the letter 'ñ'). As most Spanish schoolchildren will spell out a word using the word 'acento' to indicate the written mark, we do the same to avoid confusion. It's worth remembering, though, that the acento is often referred to as the 'tilde' in serious articles on the Spanish language.

Lesson One — palabras agudas

Don't put a written accent on word whose stress is on their last syllable (the Real Academia de la Lengua Espanola call them "palabras agudas") unless they end with a consonant other than n or s.

If they end with an n or an s, or with a vowel, you need to put the written accent on the stressed vowel.

Examples:

  • comer
  • profesor
  • Madrid
  • animal

BUT

  • comió
  • será
  • comilón
  • organización
  • aquí
  • estás
  • mamá
  • sofás

Palabras agudas don't need acentos if:

they end with a consonant other than n or s

otherwise they do

Lesson Two — palabras llanas

With words that have their stress on their second to last syllable (The Real Academia call these ones "palabras graves" or "palabras llanas"), the rule is the other way around: they should be given a written accent if they end with a consonant other than n or s. And if they end with an n, an s or a vowel, then you shouldn't mark the stress in writing.

Examples:

  • azúcar
  • lápiz
  • cárcel
  • fútbol

BUT

  • barco
  • cama
  • dinero
  • naranjas
  • galleta
  • alguien
  • terminan
  • Carmen

There is, however, an exception you'll probably need to remember: with palabras llanas that end with n or s, if the n or s is preceded by a consonant, then you'll need to mark the stressed syllable with a written accent after all.

This often affects words with a recent origin in a foreign language (or Latin).

For example:

  • bíceps/li>
  • wéstern
  • cómics
need

Palabras agudas need acentos if:

they end with a consonant other than n or s

otherwise they don't

Exception: if the last letter is n or s and the second-last letter is a consonant, they need an acento on the second-last syllable

Lesson Three — palabras esdrújulas and sobreesdrújulas

Words with a stress on their third from last syllable (called "palabras esdrújulas" by the Real Academia) always have a written accent.

Examples:

  • guapísimo
  • pájaro
  • célula
  • esdrújula

Words with the stress before the third-last syllable (palabras sobreesdrújulas) also need their stressed syllable to be marked with a written accent.

As always, though, (¿cómo no?) there is an exception. Luckily for us, it's pretty easy to remember: adverbs ending with -mente don't need a written accent unless the adjective that forms their root needs one. Effectively what the rule means is that you should add -mente as required by the applicable rules, leaving any written accent that was already on the root untouched.

SO ...
  • velozmente
  • claramente
BUT ...
  • simpáticamente (because simpática – as a palabra esdrújula – already has an accent!)
  • útilmente (because, útil – as a palabra llana ending with a consonant other than n or s – already has an accent!)
  • cortésmente (because, cortés – as a palabra aguda ending with an s – already needs an accent!)

Palabras esdrújulas and sobreesdrújulas always need an acento!

Exception: words ending with -mente only need an acento if their base word needs an acento

Lesson Four — distinguishing homonyms

Some very commonly used (and mostly short) words have a written accent when used in one sense and have none when used in another.

The following are some of the most common ones:

Examples:

  • dé (from the verb dar) vs. de (of)
  • sé (from the verb ser) vs. se (reflexive pronoun)
  • él (as a pronoun) vs. el (the definite article)
  • tú (you) vs tu (your)
  • mí (me) vs mi (my)
  • té (tea) vs. te (you)
  • qué (as a question word – in direct or indirect questions) vs. que (as a relative pronoun)
  • quién/quiénes (as a question word) vs. quien/quienes (as a relative pronoun)
  • cuándo vs. cuando (see above)
  • cuánto/cuántos vs. cuanto/cuandos
    cuánta/cuántas vs. cuanta/cuantas (see above)
  • cuál/cuáles vs. cual/cuales (see above)
  • por qué vs. porque (see above)
  • cómo vs. como (like, as in similar to)
  • más (more) vs. mas (but)
  • solo (alone) vs. sólo (only)
  • sí (yes) vs. si (if)
    • li> aún (still, even) vs. aun (despite)

Question words need acentos

Pronouns need acentos if they have homophones meaning something different.

You need to remember the other words that have acentos to distinguish them from their homophones.

Lesson Five — Dipthongs, tripthongs and hiatuses

The first thing to say under this category is that dipthongs and tripthongs follow the same rules about when to add a written acento as any other vowels. But when there are two or three vowels written one after another, on which of them do you put the acento?

Well the key to this problem lies in remembering that Spanish grammaticians split the vowels into strong ones (a, e and o) and weak ones (i and u).

Dipthongs are always either a combination of two weak vowels or of one weak and one strong vowel. Two strong vowels written together are always pronounced as two separate syllables (sea).

Tripthongs are always a combination of weak-strong-weak vowels. Any other combination of three vowels together will automatically be spoken using at least two syllables.

So (brace yourself!), the rules on where to put your acento, if one is needed, are as follows:

  • in dipthongs with two weak vowels, put the acento on the second of them (veintiún)
  • in dipthongs with one weak and one strong vowel, put the acento on the strong one (com-ió)
  • in tripthongs, put the acento on the strong vowel, which will always be in the middle (com-iáis)
  • if you place an acento anywhere else, it will
    1. break the combination into two syllables
    2. put the word's stress on the syllable you've marked with the acento (sa-lí-ais)

Dipthongs and tripthongs follow the same rules on written acentos as any other vowels.

Dipthong two weak vowels: acento (where needed) on second vowel

Otherwise put the acento (where needed) on the strong vowel.

Stressed tripthongs always have their acento (where needed) on the strong vowel
